The Tension

Go Cheese Lovers Day is January 20th. It is not a well-known occasion.

The brief was to make it feel like one — to own the day digitally, drive brand awareness, and create enough engagement that people actually participated rather than scrolling past.

The campaign had to run across four platforms simultaneously, with five separate contest posts going live at two-hour intervals through the day. Each contest tied to a specific GO Cheese product and a character from the JustCheese League universe the creative team had built. Timing had to be exact — too early and the audience wasn't primed, too late and the momentum died.

Coordinating the UX and development (the microsite), influencer management, paid media, organic content, and client approval across four stakeholders — all running in parallel — was where most of the delivery pressure sat.

What I Did

My job was to make sure every moving part moved at the right time

Before launch: built the campaign timeline across all workstreams, created the RACI matrix, managed the microsite delivery in parallel with campaign asset production, ran the approval workflow with four client stakeholders, and coordinated the student influencer programme with Frapp.

On launch day: monitored all five contest posts in real time as they went live at two-hour intervals, managed the Twitter influencer activity to maintain trending momentum, coordinated Instagram Stories between contest windows to keep awareness high, and kept the client updated throughout the day.

Post-campaign: delivered the full performance report, documented learnings, and built the case study materials for the agency portfolio.

A campaign like this lives or dies on sequencing. The content was strong. The creative was strong. But none of it would have landed if the timing was off or one platform fell behind and disrupted the others. The project management was the invisible layer that made the visible work possible.

What I Learned

Timing is a deliverable

For high-volume single-day campaigns, a project timeline is not enough. You need a minute-by-minute execution schedule. Every post has a window. Miss it and the next one is already due.

This was also the campaign where I really understood the difference between managing a project and managing a live campaign. In a project, you plan ahead. In a live campaign, you're making real-time decisions while the results are already coming in.

Takeaway for other teams

Build a minute-by-minute execution schedule for single-day campaigns. Assign one person per platform for the day. Decide your escalation path for real-time issues before launch — not during it.
