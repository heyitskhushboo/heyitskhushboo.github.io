The Tension

The same system had to serve two very different people

HealthLink BC functions as the main digital entry point into BC's healthcare system. It supports the 8-1-1 phone service, connects people to care programs, and acts as the trusted source for medically reviewed health information.

A nurse calling 8-1-1 for a clinical reference needs speed. A parent looking up a child's symptoms at midnight needs clarity. The same system was being asked to serve both — and it was built for neither.

The content itself was accurate. But it was structured the way a Ministry thinks about health information, not the way a person searches for it. Navigation reflected internal organisational logic. Some pages mixed audiences — clinical detail alongside plain language guidance — with no clear distinction.

And layered on top: multiple approval chains, cross-ministry dependencies, clinical sign-off requirements, and a CMS migration that had to happen while the live site kept running.

Building enough trust that clinical experts would accept plain language edits — without feeling like the medical accuracy was being sacrificed — took time and patience. That was some of the most important work I did.

What I Did

My role was to keep things accurate, accessible, and moving

Content coordination and migration — tracked dependencies between clinical approval and web publishing across thousands of pages in Drupal, so nothing got stuck at the wrong stage or went live without sign-off.

Quality assurance — checked pages against plain language and WCAG standards, not just as a checklist but reading them the way a real user would. If something was technically compliant but still confusing, that was still a problem.

Usability testing support — facilitated sessions with Ministry of Health staff and helped synthesise what came back into something the team could act on. Design teams used prototypes, ran scenarios, and iterated based on what participants struggled with.

Stakeholder communication — coordinated across Ministry of Health, PHSA, clinical teams, UX, and development. Often this meant translating between what a clinician needed to say and what a user needed to understand. Two valid things that don't always look the same on a page.

Before vs After

What changed for the user

Before
  • Navigation reflected Ministry org structure, not user mental models
  • Pages mixed clinical and public audiences without distinction
  • ImmunizeBC content lived on a separate site
  • Health Connect Registry not easily discoverable
  • Public Health Alerts buried, not surfaced on homepage
  • Content migration managed manually with no structured tracking
After
  • Restructured information architecture based on how people search for health info
  • Improved Find Care section with Health Connect Registry visible
  • ImmunizeBC content consolidated into main HealthLink BC portal
  • Public Health Alerts surfaced on the homepage
  • WCAG 2.1 AA compliance across the platform
  • Drupal CMS upgraded and structured for easier ongoing maintenance

At a broader level, the redesign supported what the BC Government describes as "a blueprint for connected healthcare" — linking patients more effectively to providers, and making the digital front door of the health system actually work the way a front door should.

Published source: Improving HealthLink BC's website for everyone's needs — BC Digital Government, March 2025

What I Learned

Clarity has to be built into the system, not added later

Content structure is a core part of UX, not something you sort out after the design is done. The pages that caused the most confusion weren't poorly written — they were organised for the wrong audience.

Working in a regulated environment also taught me that the hardest part is rarely the work itself. It's building enough credibility with subject matter experts that they trust you to make their content clearer without making it wrong.

Takeaway for other teams

If you're working on a large content migration, audit for audience first. Who is each page actually for? What do they need to do with this information? That single question will reorganise more content than any style guide.
