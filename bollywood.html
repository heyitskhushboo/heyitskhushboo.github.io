What I'm describing here is the PM layer that made the execution actually work.

The Environment

Entertainment marketing runs on deadlines that do not move

A film has a release date. That date does not change. If an asset is late, an influencer drops out, or a client approval gets stuck, the campaign still has to go live — on time, on all platforms, at the same moment.

Between 2019 and 2020, the context shifted dramatically. Chhichhore and Panga were standard theatrical campaigns — long lead times, physical events, coordinated nationwide launches. Then COVID-19 arrived, cinemas closed, and three of the five films became OTT-first releases on Disney+ Hotstar. The entire delivery model changed overnight.

Each film brought a different kind of pressure. Managing all five taught me more about risk, speed, and client communication than almost anything else in my career.

What the PM Role Actually Looked Like

Across all five campaigns

Strategic Planning & Client Liaison

Primary point of contact between production houses and the agency. Translated creative briefs into execution plans. Managed expectation alignment and kept approval cycles from becoming delays.

Execution & Coordination

Led cross-functional teams across design, copy, social media, and media planning. Built delivery schedules and milestone trackers. Kept dependencies visible so nothing fell through.

Risk & Crisis Management

Maintained risk and issue logs across all campaigns. For Sadak 2, ran active brand safety monitoring and real-time sentiment tracking. Made fast calls on escalation without waiting for the issue to worsen.

Performance & Reporting

Monitored campaign KPIs in real time and provided regular client updates. Delivered post-campaign analysis reports and documented learnings for future entertainment campaigns.

What I Learned

Crisis management is just project management under pressure

The tools are the same. What changes is how fast you have to use them and how clearly you have to communicate.

Between Lootcase and Sadak 2 releasing within weeks of each other in 2020, I managed two campaigns in completely opposite emotional environments simultaneously — one light and viral, one under sustained negative scrutiny. That contrast was the best possible education in reading a situation and adjusting without overreacting.

The other thing these campaigns confirmed: delivery discipline is what makes large-scale creative work succeed. The ideas matter. The execution makes them real.

Takeaway for other teams

Build your risk log before you need it. For high-visibility campaigns, the question is not whether something will go wrong — it is whether your team has already discussed what to do when it does. Pre-agreed escalation paths save hours when you're running at full speed.
